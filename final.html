Autonomous Vehicle Crashes

How dangerous are automated cars?
Jessica Klein

National Highway Traffic Safety Administration

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) has been actively extending its reach into the area of autonomous driving systems (ADS) and automated driver assistance systems (ADAS). Through a series of standing general orders (SGOs), the agency has mandated reporting obligations for certain crashes involving these technologies on public roads. The recent extension of these orders, discussed in Foley and Lardner LLP (an innovative technology law firm) not only emphasizes NHTSA's stance but also reflects the urgency of ensuring public safety. NHTSA wants to ensure safety in a world where autonomous vehicles are increasingly widespread.

Neil Dold, a lead engineer at the Department of Transportation for NHTSA, emphasized the agency’s commitment to transparency regarding the data collected through these orders. "But, you know, the primary motivation was for our specific purposes as a regulatory agency to help us better regulate these technologies," Dold explained. "It's because we understand that the public, you know, wants some amount of transparency and there's a high degree of interest in this."

The Issue With Autonomous Vehicles

While vehicle manufacturers work to add technologies aimed at enhancing vehicle safety, the annual fatality statistics published by the NHTSA since approximately 2015 show the need for continued action. In the span of 2021 to 2023, NHTSA's estimates that nearly 43,000 lives were lost in vehicle-related accidents in 2021 alone. This figure marks a significant increase from the previous year, highlighting the urgency for continued efforts to enhance road safety. In response to this concerning trend, the Agency maintains an active role in enforcing safety standards, investigating potential issues, and evaluating new technologies.

Important Findings and Their Limitations

Geographic Locations

One striking finding is the geographic area of autonomous crashes. Over 50% of autonomous crashes recorded between 2021 and 2023 occurred in San Francisco, California. This concentration suggests that certain regions may present challenges or conditions that impact the vehicle’s performance. San Francisco's densely populated streets, traffic patterns, and road layouts make a complex environment for autonomous vehicles to navigate. The city's mix of pedestrian and vehicle traffic leads to significant challenges for the sensor and navigation systems of autonomous vehicles.

San Francisco also is a hub for technology development, attracting companies and startups to test and showcase their autonomous technologies on its streets. The concentration of autonomous crashes in this tech-forward city shows the rapid pace of innovation in the autonomous vehicle industry. It also highlights the need for testing protocols and regulatory measures to help safe integration of autonomous vehicles into urban environments.

Weather Conditions

Surprisingly, weather conditions seem to have little effect on the occurrence of autonomous crashes. Despite common assumptions about the impact of adverse weather, over 90% of autonomous crashes occurred on dry surfaces. This occurrence leads to ideas about different seasonal factors that might impact driving conditions. For instance, September normally marks the end of summer vacation in many regions, leading to increased traffic as people return to school and work routines. September often has plenty of events, which may result in worse traffic conditions. These factors could put pressure on autonomous vehicle systems, testing their ability to adapt to dynamic driving environments and respond to unforeseen circumstances.

Types of Crashes

Another significant finding from the data is the common factor of collisions between autonomous vehicles and passenger vehicles. Almost one out of every three autonomous crashes involved passenger vehicles, highlighting the issue of interactions between autonomous and human-operated vehicles on the road. This observation shows the complexities in the interaction of autonomous and human-driven vehicles on the road. It suggests that despite advancements in autonomous technology, challenges exist in integration and safe interaction between these two types of vehicles.

The common nature of collisions between autonomous and passenger vehicles also raises important questions about factors contributing to these incidents. It could come from differences in driving behavior, anticipation, and decision-making processes. Human drivers often rely on visual cues, gestures, and non-verbal communication to navigate traffic, where autonomous vehicles rely on sensors, algorithms, and pre-programmed responses. As well, different levels of awareness, adaptability, and situational understanding between human drivers and autonomous systems may lead to misunderstandings or misinterpretations on the road, increasing the likelihood of collisions. Issues such as sensor limitations, technological glitches, or unforeseen environmental conditions further make these crashes more likely.

Limitations

However, it's important to recognize the limitations in the data and findings presented. While the crash reports provide valuable information, they may not capture the full range of autonomous vehicle incidents. Reporting requirements, technological limitations, and variations in data quality across different manufacturers could influence the accuracy and completeness of the dataset. As well, Dodd explained that testing is done in certain areas due to certain permissions or tax benefits.

As the autonomous vehicle landscape continues to evolve, ongoing research and collaboration will be essential to address the challenges and opportunities presented by this transformative technology. NHTSA's commitment to providing the public data sets an example for other agencies worldwide, paving the way for a safer and more sustainable future of transportation
